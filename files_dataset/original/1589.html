Here's a summary: The idea of creating a device that could produce light using electricity had been around for some time, but it wasn't until the late 1870s that the first practical incandescent light bulb was invented. Thomas Edison, an American inventor and businessman, is often credited with developing the first commercially viable light bulb. Edison's breakthrough came after years of experimentation with various materials, including carbon filaments. He discovered that by using a carbon filament in a vacuum-sealed glass bulb, he could create a light source that could be powered by a direct current (DC) electrical supply. This was a significant improvement over earlier experiments, which used an alternating current (AC) supply and were prone to burning out quickly due to the high voltage required. On December 24, 1879, Edison officially patented his light bulb design, which included a carbon filament inside a glass bulb filled with nitrogen gas. The bulb was powered by a DC electrical current, and it produced a warm, yellowish light that was relatively dim compared to modern standards. However, it was still a revolutionary invention that marked the beginning of a new era in electric lighting. Over the next several years, Edison refined his light bulb design and developed new manufacturing techniques that made it possible to mass-produce these devices. By the early 1900s, light bulbs had become a common household item, making life easier and safer for people around the world. Today, we take for granted the ability to flip a switch and have light available at any hour of the day or night, thanks to the pioneering work of Thomas Edison and other inventors who followed in his footsteps.
