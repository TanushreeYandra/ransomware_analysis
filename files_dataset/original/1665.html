Here's a brief summary: The idea of using electricity to produce light dates back to the 18th century, but it wasn't until the late 19th century that a durable and practical incandescent light bulb was invented. In 1879, Thomas Edison developed the first commercially practical incandescent light bulb, which used a carbon filament to produce light when an electric current passed through it. This breakthrough made electric lighting a viable alternative to traditional gaslights and kerosene lamps, which were expensive and produced flickering light. Edison's design improved upon earlier experiments by inventors such as Joseph Swan and Hiram Maxim, who had also worked on incandescent light bulbs. Edison's bulb was more efficient than previous designs because it used a higher voltage (240 volts) and a longer carbon filament, which increased its lifespan and reduced the risk of fires caused by short-circuiting. Over time, improvements were made to the light bulb design, including the use of tungsten filaments instead of carbon, which further increased efficiency and lifespan. Today, light bulbs come in a variety of shapes, sizes, and wattages, and are powered by electricity from mains or battery sources. In addition to providing brightness and convenience for households and businesses, the light bulb has played a significant role in shaping modern society. It has enabled people to work and study during the night, expanded access to education and healthcare, and contributed to economic growth and development.
