Here's a brief summary: The idea of creating a light source that could be powered by electricity had been around for some time, but it wasn't until the mid-19th century that someone finally succeeded. In 1879, a British inventor named Joseph Swan created the first incandescent light bulb, which used a carbon filament to produce light when an electric current was passed through it. However, these early light bulbs were not very practical or efficient, as they burned out quickly and produced only a dim glow. It wasn't until 1880 that Thomas Edison, an American inventor, improved upon Swan's design by using a carbon filament in a vacuum-sealed glass bulb. This design significantly increased the lifespan and brightness of the light bulb, making it more practical for use in homes and businesses. Edison's light bulb was powered by a direct current (DC) electricity source, which was readily available at the time. Over the next few decades, improvements were made to the light bulb design, including the development of more efficient filaments and the introduction of alternating current (AC) electricity. These advances allowed for the widespread adoption of electric lighting in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, revolutionizing the way people lived and worked. Today, light bulbs are an essential part of our daily lives, powering everything from home lighting to streetlights to electronic devices.
