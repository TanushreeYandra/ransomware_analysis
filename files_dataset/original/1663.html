In 1845, a British inventor named Frederick de Moleyns patented an design for a "electric lamp," which consisted of a carbon filament suspended in a vacuum inside a glass cylinder. However, it wasn't until the late 1870s that the first practical incandescent light bulb was developed. In 1879, Thomas Edison, an American inventor and businessman, began working on a more efficient light bulb design. He experimented with different materials, including carbon filaments, and discovered that a carbon filament in a vacuum inside a glass cylinder could be heated to produce light. Edison's light bulb used a direct current (DC) electricity source and had a lifespan of around 12 hours. Edison's breakthrough was quickly followed by other inventors and companies, such as Joseph Swan and Hiram Maxim, who also developed incandescent light bulbs. By the early 20th century, light bulbs had become widely available and affordable, making nighttime activities possible without the need for candles or gas lamps. This led to significant changes in daily life, including increased productivity and leisure time, as well as new forms of entertainment and socializing. Today, light bulbs are still based on the same principle of passing electricity through a thin filament to produce light. However, there have been many advancements in technology, leading to more energy-efficient and longer-lasting bulbs. LED lights, for example, use semiconductors instead of filaments and last up to 25 times longer than traditional incandescent bulbs.
