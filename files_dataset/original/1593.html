However, it wasn't until the late 19th century that a significant breakthrough was made. In 1879, Thomas Edison, an American inventor, developed the first practical incandescent light bulb. Edison's light bulb used a carbon filament, which was heated by an electric current passing through it. This created a warm glow that could be controlled by a switch. The bulb was encased in a glass cylinder filled with nitrogen gas to prevent it from exploding when turned on. Over the next few years, Edison and his team improved upon their design, developing a longer-last-ing bulb with a more efficient filament. They also developed a central power station to provide electricity to homes and businesses, making the light bulb a viable alternative to candles and gas lamps. In 1882, Joseph Swan, a British inventor, independently developed a light bulb using a carbon filament. He had been experimenting with different materials and discovered that a carbon filament would burn brighter and longer than other materials. The light bulb quickly became an essential part of daily life, providing a reliable source of light for work, study, and leisure activities. It also spurred technological innovations, such as the development of electrical grids and home appliances. Today, the light bulb has evolved into various forms, including compact fluorescent bulbs (CFLs), LED bulbs, and even solar-powered lights. These newer technologies are more energy-efficient and environmentally friendly than traditional incandescent bulbs, and they continue to improve with advancements in technology.
