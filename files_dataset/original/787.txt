Indigenous Peoples (10,000 BCE - 1492 CE): The first inhabitants of North America were indigenous peoples who migrated from Asia across the Bering Land Bridge around 15,000 years ago. These groups developed unique cultures, languages, and societies, including the Inuit, Aleut, Iroquois Confederacy, Sioux, Apache, Cherokee, Maya, Aztecs, and Incas. They lived across what is now Canada, the United States, Mexico, Central America, and South America.

European Exploration and Settlement (1492-1776): In 1492, Christopher Columbus landed in the Caribbean, initiating European exploration and colonization of the Americas. Over the next two centuries, European powers such as Spain, France, Britain, and Portugal established colonies along the eastern coast of North America, displacing native populations and exploiting their resources. Conflicts between colonizers and natives escalated, culminating in the American Revolution (1775-1783), which led to the establishment of the United States of America.

American Revolution and Formation of the USA (1776-1789): In 1776, the American Revolutionary War ended with the signing of the Treaty of Paris, recognizing the independence of the United States. The new nation faced challenges in its early years, including the Articles of Confederation, which proved ineffective in governing the diverse states. In 1787, the Constitutional Convention was held, resulting in the drafting of the United States Constitution, which remains the foundation of the country's government today.

Westward Expansion and Manifest Destiny (1803-1860): From the early 19th century onwards, the United States expanded westward through treaties, purchases, and conflicts with Native Americans. The concept of "Manifest Destiny" emerged during this period, emphasizing the belief that the U.S. had a divine right to expand its territory across North America. This era saw the Louisiana Purchase (1803), the Mexican-American War (1846), and the California Gold Rush (1848).

Civil War and Reconstruction (1861-1877): Tensions between slave-holding Southern states and free-soil Northern states culminated in the Civil War (1861-1865). After four bloody years, the Union emerged victorious, but at great cost. The subsequent Reconstruction Era aimed to rebuild the South and establish civil rights for former slaves, but it was marred by violence, corruption, and political divisions.

Industrialization and Immigration (1870-1914): During this period, the United States experienced rapid industrialization, urbanization, and immigration. New technologies, transportation networks, and business practices transformed the economy and society, leading to increased wealth and prosperity for some, while others struggled to keep up.

World Wars and the Great Depression (1914-1945): The United States entered both World War I (1917-1918) and World War II (1941-1945) as a major ally, playing a crucial role in both conflicts. Following WWII, the United States experienced an economic boom known as the "Golden Age," but also witnessed the Great Depression (1929-1941) and the rise of the New Deal under President Franklin D. Roosevelt.

Cold War and Civil Rights Movement (1945-1989): After WWII, the United States emerged as one of the world's superpowers, engaging in a decades-long Cold War standoff with the Soviet Union. At home, the Civil Rights Movement (1955-1968) advocated for racial equality and social justice, achieving significant progress through nonviolent protests and legislative action.
