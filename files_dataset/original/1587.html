Here's a brief summary: The first incandescent light bulb was invented by Thomas Edison in 1879. Prior to this, people relied on gas-filled lanterns or candles for indoor lighting. Edison's breakthrough came when he discovered that by using a carbon filament in an electric current, he could create a glowing light that would last for hours without burning out. Edison experimented with various materials for the filament, including cotton thread, hemp, and bamboo. He eventually settled on a carbonized cotton thread that was strong enough to support the electrical current but fragile enough to burn out easily when the power was turned off. This innovation allowed for the development of the first practical incandescent light bulbs, which were marketed as "electric lamps." Over time, other inventors improved upon Edison's design, developing more efficient and longer-lasting light bulbs. Today, we have access to a wide range of light sources, from compact fluorescents to LED lights, all thanks to the pioneering work of Edison and other inventors in the field of electrical engineering.
