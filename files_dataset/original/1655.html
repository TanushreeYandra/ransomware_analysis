Here's a summary: The idea for the light bulb was first proposed by Humphry Davy, an English chemist, in 1809. However, it wasn't until 1879 that Thomas Edison developed the first practical incandescent light bulb. Edison used a carbon filament to create a hot, glowing wire when an electric current passed through it. This allowed the filament to burn for hours without burning out, making it a viable option for lighting homes and businesses. Edison's design improved upon earlier experiments by inventors such as Joseph Swan and Hiram Maxim, who had also worked on incandescent lighting. Edison's bulb was powered by a direct current (DC) electrical supply, which proved more reliable than the alternating current (AC) systems being developed at the time. Over the next several decades, improvements were made to Edison's original design, including the use of longer-lasting and more efficient filaments, as well as the development of new materials like tungsten and halogen. These advances led to the widespread adoption of light bulbs in homes, offices, and public spaces, transforming the way people lived and worked. Today, light bulbs are an essential part of modern life, providing bright, reliable lighting for everything from reading and working to entertainment and socializing.
