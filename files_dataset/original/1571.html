In 1879, Thomas Edison, an American inventor and businessman, developed the first commercially practical incandescent light bulb. Edison's breakthrough came after years of experimentation with different materials and designs. He discovered that by using a carbon filament in a vacuum-sealed glass cylinder, he could create a long-last-ing light source that could be powered by a simple electrical circuit. The filament was heated by an electric current, producing a warm glow of light. Over the next few years, Edison and his team worked to improve the design and efficiency of their light bulbs. They developed new materials and techniques, such as using a tungsten filament instead of carbon, which greatly increased the lifespan of the bulb. By 1882, Edison had created a light bulb that could burn for over 12 hours on a single charge. The invention of the light bulb revolutionized the world in many ways. It made possible the widespread use of electricity in homes and businesses, leading to greater convenience, safety, and productivity. It also enabled the development of many other technologies, including telephones, radios, and televisions. Today, light bulbs are a common household item, used in everything from lamps and chandeliers to flashlights and streetlights.
