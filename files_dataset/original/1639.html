In 1802, the Italian physicist Alessandro Volta invented the first battery, which produced a steady electric current. This breakthrough led to the development of electrochemical cells, which could produce an electric current through chemical reactions. In the mid-19th century, British inventor Humphry Davy discovered that an electric current could be used to excite gases and create light. He demonstrated this by passing an electric current through a gas-filled tube filled with carbon, which emitted light. However, the light was very weak and not practical for everyday use. It wasn't until the late 1870s that Thomas Edison, an American inventor and businessman, made significant progress towards creating a practical light bulb. Edison experimented with different materials, including carbon filaments, and discovered that a carbon filament wrapped in a cylinder of carbon powder could sustain an electric current long enough to produce a stable light source. On October 21, 1879, Edison created the first commercially viable incandescent light bulb, which used a carbon filament to produce light. Edison's invention quickly gained popularity, and the light bulb became a standard fixture in homes and businesses around the world. Over time, improvements were made to the design and materials used in light bulbs, leading to longer lifetimes, brighter lights, and more efficient energy consumption.
