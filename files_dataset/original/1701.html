In 1879, Thomas Edison developed the first commercially practical incandescent light bulb, which used a carbon filament to produce light when an electric current passed through it. Edison's design improved upon earlier experiments by inventors such as Joseph Swan and Hiram Maxim, who had also worked on incandescent lighting. Edison's breakthrough came after years of experimentation and refinement of his design. He discovered that by using a carbon filament in a vacuum-sealed glass bulb, he could achieve a stable and efficient source of light. His light bulb used a direct current (DC) electricity supply, which provided a consistent flow of electricity to the filament, allowing it to glow for hours without overheating or burning out. Over time, the design of the light bulb evolved to improve efficiency and reduce costs. Other inventors and companies developed new materials and techniques, such as using tungsten instead of carbon for the filament, and developing more efficient vacuum pumps to remove air from the bulb. Today, light bulbs are made with a variety of materials, including LEDs (light-emitting diodes), which are even more energy-efficient than traditional incandescent bulbs.
