However, it wasn't until the late 1870s that two inventors, Thomas Edison and Joseph Swan, made significant progress towards developing a practical incandescent light bulb. Edison's early experiments involved experimenting with different materials, such as carbon filaments, to create a reliable and efficient light source. He developed a vacuum pump that allowed him to remove the air from inside the bulb, which greatly improved its efficiency. In 1879, Edison created the first commercially viable incandescent light bulb, which used a carbon filament and produced a warm white light. Swan, on the other hand, had been working on his own version of the incandescent light bulb since the 1850s. His design used a carbon filament surrounded by a nitrogen-filled glass bulb, which provided better insulation against heat loss. In 1882, Edison established the first commercial electrical power station in New York City, which provided electricity for the lights in his office building. This marked the beginning of widespread electric lighting in homes and businesses, making life easier and safer at night. Over time, the design of the light bulb evolved to use more efficient materials, such as tungsten, and new technologies like fluorescent lighting were developed. Today, there are many types of light bulbs available, including LED bulbs, which are energy-efficient and last much longer than traditional incandescent bulbs.
