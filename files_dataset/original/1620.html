Here's a summary: The idea of using electricity to create light dates back to the early 19th century, but it wasn't until the late 1870s that a breakthrough was made. In 1878, Thomas Edison, an American inventor and businessman, developed the first practical incandescent light bulb. He created a vacuum pump to remove air from a carbon filament, which greatly increased its lifespan and efficiency. Edison's design used a carbon filament heated by an electric current, producing a warm white light. His light bulb consisted of a glass cylinder filled with carbon filaments, a metal wire mesh for support, and a wooden base for stability. A central power station provided electricity to the light bulbs through a system of wires. On October 21, 1879, Edison publicly demonstrated his light bulb at his laboratory in Menlo Park, New Jersey, to a group of investors and reporters. The demonstration marked the beginning of the electrical age and paved the way for modern electricity distribution systems. Over time, improvements were made to Edison's original design. Other inventors experimented with different materials like tungsten and quartz, leading to more efficient and longer-lasting light bulbs. Today, light bulbs are available in various shapes, sizes, colors, and energy efficiencies, offering versatility and convenience for households, businesses, and industries around the world.
