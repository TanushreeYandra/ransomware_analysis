However, it wasn't until the late 1800s that a British inventor named Joseph Swan developed a working incandescent light bulb. Swan's design used a carbon filament in a vacuum-sealed glass bulb to produce light when an electric current was applied. Although he didn't patent his invention, Swan demonstrated his bulb to a group of investors in 1878. They were impressed but ultimately rejected the idea as impractical for commercial use. Undeterred, another inventor named Thomas Edison took up the challenge and improved upon Swan's design. In 1879, Edison developed a light bulb that could burn for hours using a carbon filament. He also developed a central power station to provide electricity to homes and businesses, making the light bulb a practical reality. Edison's light bulb quickly gained popularity, and by the early 1900s, they had become a standard fixture in homes and offices around the world. Since then, the technology has continued to evolve, with advancements in materials science leading to more efficient and longer-lasting bulbs. Today, there are many types of light bulbs available, including LED, fluorescent, and halogen bulbs, each with its own unique characteristics and advantages.
