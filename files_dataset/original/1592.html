Here's a brief summary: The idea for the light bulb was first proposed by Humphry Davy, a British chemist, in 1809. However, it wasn't until the late 1870s that two inventors, Thomas Edison and Joseph Swan, independently developed working prototypes of the incandescent light bulb. Edison's design used a carbon filament heated by an electric current to produce light. He experimented with various materials, including carbonized cotton thread, until he found one that could sustain the heat long enough to provide a reliable source of light. In 1879, Edison created the first commercial light bulb factory in Menlo Park, New Jersey, where he produced over 500 light bulbs per day. Swan, on the other hand, had been working on his own version of the light bulb since the early 1860s. His design used a carbon arc lamp, which was more efficient than Edison's filament-based bulb but not as practical for everyday use. In the years following their respective breakthroughs, both Edison and Swan continued to improve upon their designs, leading to significant advancements in electrical engineering and the widespread adoption of electric lighting. Today, light bulbs are a ubiquitous part of modern life, providing brightness and safety in homes, offices, and public spaces around the world.
